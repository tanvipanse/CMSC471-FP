Hot Zones: Wildfire Risk Map

Year by year, the United States gets plagued by fires. To be more specific, between the years of 1992 and 2015, the US was riddled with over 1.5 million wildfires,
but their impact varied dramatically based on location. This series of visualizations is meant to allow users to explore the when, where, and primarily the why of American wildfires.   


The big picture: the U.S. fire landscape.

As you drag the slider on the bottom, allow the map to take you through the years, and observe the fire concentrations as they occur.
Below the map is a legend, which describes the specifics of how color and circle size demonstrate the severity (acres burned) of the fire.
Hovering over each circle draws in a tooltip, explaining the when, where, and how of each fire.

        
    
       
  
  
    Legend     
    
  
Now that you’ve had the opportunity to play with the map, we’d like to call something to your attention (if you haven’t already noticed it).

Wildfires are not random.

After some examination, you’ll quickly begin to notice crucial patterns.

The largest, most severe fires frequently blaze in the west. The southeast sees fires as well, but they are much smaller, and highly concentrated.
In certain years, we see particular states light up–for example, observe Oregon in 2012. You’ll notice large red and black circles along the southeastern border.

  

We’ve investigated size, so let’s move on to the cause–what is causing these wildfires to occur?

Underneath, we have a dynamic bar chart that reveals the major causes of fires each year. It is updated as the map animation is paused on a certain year.
Hit play, and watch the animation unfold 3 decades worth of wildfires across the country. Do you notice any alarming trends?

       
  
    

Fire Causes (by Count) for Year: 2020

       
       

As you pause on a year, you can click on any cause that piques your interest to reveal a state-by-state heat map that shows how many fires of that particular cause occurred in that year.
Darker colors represent high fire counts, as shown in the key on the left.

    

Heatmap by Cause and State

       
     

  This final visualization is truly meant to tie the story together. The map and bar chart together paint an easily digestible view of the problem.
But understanding the problem is only the first step. We use this heatmap to demonstrate where targeted interventions can make the biggest difference.

The viewpoint now shifts from purely asking what happened, to understanding where to ask. This is information that could benefit policymakers and climate activists
in their journey to mitigate the wildfires that blaze across the country. The addition of this feature is important as a tool to allow support for decision making and action.
